Project Overview

This project focused on designing and building a safe that could be unlocked using either a fingerprint sensor or a four-digit touchscreen code. The system combined an Arduino Mega, fingerprint authentication, a touchscreen interface, door sensors, solenoids, an electromagnet, and a buzzer into one working mechatronic system. The final design was not just about making the safe open and close, but about managing the interaction between hardware, software logic, power distribution, and physical packaging in a reliable way.

One of the most rewarding parts of this project was seeing a full system come together from many smaller design decisions. The safe had to interpret user input, verify access, actuate internal locking hardware, monitor the door state, and trigger an alarm if the door stayed open too long. That meant the project required careful coordination between electronics, embedded logic, fabrication, and repeated troubleshooting through each stage of development.

My Role in the Project

In this project, I handled the hands-on development side of the build. I took the lead on prototyping, testing, assembly, CAD modeling, repeated fitment checks, repeated system testing, and much of the research and design work that shaped the final version. That included working through how the door would physically package the electronics, how the locking hardware would fit, and how the system would behave once all of the components were integrated into one assembly.

My teammates supported the project by helping with documentation and purchasing, which made it possible for me to stay focused on iteration and implementation. That workflow let us move faster while still keeping the project organized. It also reflected how engineering teams often work in practice, where some members keep procurement and written deliverables moving while others push the prototype itself through design changes, integration, and testing.

I am also currently working on a V2 of this project in my free time. The goal is to improve packaging, alignment, power delivery, and overall reliability based on the issues and lessons that came out of the first build.

Main areas I focused on

  • Researching the concept and selecting workable design directions
  • CAD modeling and revising parts around component fit and packaging
  • Physical prototyping and repeated troubleshooting
  • Assembly of door hardware, electronics, and system integration
  • Testing, retesting, and refining the build as problems appeared

Electronics, Logic, and Control

A major part of the project was building the logic that allowed multiple forms of access while still keeping the system secure and responsive. The safe was organized around a finite state machine that handled keypad entry, fingerprint authentication, unlocking, door-open timing, re-locking, and alarm behavior. Structuring the logic this way helped manage timing-sensitive events and made debugging far more organized as the project became more complex.

Electrically, the project required more than simply wiring everything to the Arduino. The touchscreen, fingerprint sensor, buzzer, and door sensor all had to communicate reliably with the controller, while the solenoids and electromagnet required separate power handling and switching components. The final build used a dedicated 12V supply for the actuators and a separate Arduino supply after power stability issues affected the fingerprint system during testing.

Mechanical Design and Integration

The mechanical side of the project required just as much iteration as the electronics. The door had to hold the fingerprint scanner, touchscreen, solenoids, door sensor, Arduino, and magnet while still allowing clean assembly and reliable locking. As the design evolved, multiple features had to be repositioned or reprinted to account for screw clearance, wiring space, component thickness, and the exact motion of the locking hardware.

The final design used a combination of 3D-printed parts and a wooden enclosure. That final direction came after early concept changes and fabrication constraints. Even after the parts fit individually, bringing them into one finished door assembly introduced new alignment issues that had to be corrected through more testing and adjustment.

Lessons Learned

This project taught me how quickly a mechatronics build becomes a full systems problem. Power delivery, embedded timing, sensor consistency, and mechanical tolerancing all affected each other. Even when a subsystem worked on its own, that did not guarantee it would behave the same way after full integration. Because of that, repeated testing and rework became one of the most important parts of the project.

It also reinforced how valuable early integration is. Mechanical and electrical issues tend to stay hidden until everything is brought together, and by that point every change affects several other parts of the build. The project gave me stronger experience in packaging electronics into tight spaces, thinking through real assembly constraints, and balancing design intent with what is actually practical under time limits.

Key takeaways

  • Separate actuator power and control electronics carefully from the start
  • Use structured state logic for timing-sensitive behavior
  • Expect multiple design iterations for packaging and tolerancing
  • Start full system integration earlier than feels necessary
  • Testing is not one step at the end, it is part of the design process
