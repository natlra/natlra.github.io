Project Overview

The Matcha Mill project focused on designing and building a commercial matcha mill that allows baristas to grind fresh matcha to order. The goal was to create a system that was more practical for cafe use than traditional options by making it compact, repeatable, and easier to operate in a real beverage workflow. Instead of relying on pre-ground matcha that loses flavor and aroma over time, this design aimed to support fresher preparation directly at the point of use.

The final concept combined a vertically integrated grinding system, a linear actuator driven loading mechanism, a belt-driven power transmission system, removable grinding components, and a rigid frame that could hold alignment under load. My Role in the Project

In this project, I was primarily in charge of testing, modeling, and assembly. My work centered on turning the design into something that could be physically built, adjusted, and validated through actual performance data. That meant contributing to CAD work, helping refine how the subsystems fit together, assembling mechanical components, and working through repeated iterations to make sure the mill could operate reliably under load.

The testing side was especially important because the final machine performance depended on real trial data rather than assumptions alone. A large part of my role involved evaluating how applied force, grinding speed, total grind time, and interval strategy affected particle size and consistency. That made the project a good mix of hands-on fabrication and data-based engineering decisions.

Main areas I focused on

  • CAD modeling and packaging of components into the final layout
  • Mechanical assembly of the mill and its supporting hardware
  • Building and refining the testing setup
  • Evaluating RPM, force, grind time, and interval effects
  • Helping move the design from prototype form toward a validated final system

Design Overview

The Matcha Mill was designed as a modular grinding system for single-dose cafe use. Its main subsystems included a lead-screw based axial loading system, removable grinding components, a belt-driven transmission system, and a rigid frame to maintain alignment. This modular approach made it possible to iterate on grinding geometry, loading conditions, and component packaging without completely redesigning the full machine each time.

Testing and Validation

Testing was one of the most important parts of the Matcha Mill project because the design had to prove that it could actually produce usable matcha at the target quality level. The performance studies focused on variables such as applied force, RPM, total grind time, and interval strategy. Instead of assuming one configuration would work best, the machine was evaluated through a structured test setup that allowed those variables to be compared directly.

Results

The project successfully demonstrated that the machine could produce fresh matcha in the target size range under the best operating conditions. One of the most important outcomes was identifying the lowest useful speed that still gave strong results, which helped define a practical operating point for the machine.
